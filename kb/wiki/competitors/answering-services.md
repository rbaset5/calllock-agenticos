# Answering Services: The Incumbent CallLock Must Displace

Traditional answering services are the existing paid solution contractors use when they can't answer their own phone. They're not AI startups — they're the status quo for contractors who already pay someone to answer calls.

## The Players

| Service | Model | Entry price | Billing unit | Overage | Scheduling/booking? | FSM integration? |
|---------|-------|-------------|-------------|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Smith.ai (AI)** | Hybrid AI + human | $95/mo | Per call (50/150/500 tiers: $95/$270/$800) | Per-call overage | Yes | 7,000+ tools incl. ServiceTitan, HCP |
| **Smith.ai (human)** | Live receptionists | $300+/mo | Per call | Per-call overage | Yes — qualified intake | Same integrations |

## Why Most Contractors DON'T Use Answering Services

The majority of small contractors (3-15 people) use nothing. The status quo is: owner answers when they can, voicemail when they can't. Reasons:

- **Cost.** $300-800/mo for Ruby/Nexa is hard to justify for a 5-person HVAC shop doing $500K-$1M revenue
- **Quality.** Operators handle dozens of businesses simultaneously. They read scripts. They collect wrong information (wrong system type, wrong address, wrong urgency). They can't actually book the job.
- **"Someone will call you back."** This is the death sentence. 60-80% of callers who reach voicemail hang up without leaving a message (industry estimates vary; Sameday cites 62% of calls missed overall). "We'll have someone call you back" is barely better — 35-50% of sales go to the first responder (per Lead Response Management Study, widely cited but methodology varies).
- **Peak time failures.** Storm hits, pipes freeze, 15 calls come in at once. A human answering service has finite capacity. Calls queue, customers hang up.

## Smith.ai Is the Real Competitor

Smith.ai at $95/mo for AI + human hybrid is the closest thing to CallLock in the answering service world:

**What Smith.ai does well:**
- $95/mo AI tier is cheap (cheaper than CallLock's $297)
- 7,000+ integrations including ServiceTitan and Housecall Pro
- Hybrid model: AI handles routine scheduling, humans handle emergencies and upset customers
- Captures system type, age, service category, property details
- HVAC-specific marketing page — they're actively targeting this vertical

**Where Smith.ai falls short (durable → transient):**

1. **Horizontal, not vertical (durable).** Smith.ai serves legal, healthcare, real estate, home services, and dozens of other verticals. They can't go deep on any one. CallLock is purpose-built for the trades — every workflow, every prompt, every integration decision optimized for contractors.
2. **Per-call overage pricing (durable unless they change model).** At real HVAC volume (50+ calls/week in summer), Smith.ai's $95 tier becomes $270-$800/mo. The effective cost is much higher than the entry price suggests.
3. **Finite concurrent capacity (architectural).** 15 calls hit at once during a storm — AI handles all 15 simultaneously. Human and hybrid services queue them.
4. **AI voice quality (transient — will improve).** Documented issues with "callers noticing pauses and cutouts." But Smith.ai is actively investing here. Don't build your pitch on this — it won't last.
5. **Setup complexity (transient).** "Custom AI configuration" and "white-glove implementation" suggest friction today, but this will simplify over time.

**The "why not Smith.ai?" objection:**

When a contractor asks "why not just use Smith.ai at $95/mo?", CallLock needs a clear answer:

| When CallLock wins | When Smith.ai wins | When a human service wins |
|--------------------|--------------------|--------------------------| 
| High call volume (50+/week) — per-call pricing kills Smith.ai's value | Low call volume (<50/mo) — $95 is genuinely cheap | High-value emergency calls ($5K+ installs) where trust matters most |
| After-hours/overflow — AI is always on, no staffing | Contractor wants human escalation built in | Contractor is deeply skeptical of AI on the phone |
| Storm/peak surges — unlimited simultaneous calls | Contractor already uses Smith.ai and is satisfied | Complex multi-party scheduling (subcontractors, permits) |
| Contractor on Housecall Pro/Jobber wants vertical-specific depth | Contractor needs breadth across non-phone channels (chat, email) | Bilingual markets where Nexa's Spanish capability matters |

**Cost comparison at real volume:**

| Monthly calls | Smith.ai (AI tier) | CallLock |
|--------------|-------------------|----------|
| 50 | $95 | $297 |
| 150 | $270 | $297 |
| 300 | ~$500+ (overage) | $297 |
| 500 | $800 | $297 |

At 150+ calls/month, CallLock is cheaper. A busy HVAC shop in summer easily hits this.

**Honest caveat:** This objection handling is theoretical. Zero contractors have been asked this question. The "why not Smith.ai?" answer needs to be tested in real conversations.

## Three Contractor Segments by Current Solution

### Segment A: No answering service (majority — best prospects)
- Owner answers, voicemail handles the rest
- Missing 20-60% of calls (varies wildly, unvalidated for this segment)
- Never paid for call answering before
- Price sensitivity: HIGH. $297/mo is a new expense, not a switch.
- **Pitch:** "You're losing $80 per missed call. CallLock answers every one for $297/mo — that's less than 4 missed calls."

### Segment B: Cheap answering service ($200-400/mo — good prospects)
- Using a generic service that takes messages
- Frustrated with wrong information and "someone will call you back"
- Already paying for the concept, unhappy with execution
- Price sensitivity: MODERATE. Switching, not adding a new cost.
- **Pitch:** "Your answering service takes a message. CallLock books the job."

### Segment C: Smith.ai or Ruby ($300-800/mo — harder to convert)
- Already have a working solution they're somewhat happy with
- Higher sophistication, likely larger shops
- Price sensitivity: LOW. They're already paying comparable amounts.
- **Pitch:** Has to be feature-specific: faster answer time, unlimited simultaneous calls, deeper FSM integration, or outbound capability (if Scout ships and validates).

## What This Means for CallLock's Go-to-Market

1. **Compete with voicemail first, answering services second.** The majority of target contractors use nothing. Don't over-index on beating Smith.ai — beat "I'll just check voicemail later."

2. **Smith.ai is the real answering service competitor.** Not Ruby, not Nexa. Smith.ai's $95 AI tier with 7,000+ integrations is the thing a contractor will compare CallLock to. Build the battlecard for this specific comparison.

3. **Vertical depth + after-hours is the wedge, not "books the job."** Booking is table stakes (Smith.ai books too). The durable advantage is being purpose-built for contractors: trade-specific intake, FSM-native scheduling, and after-hours specialization. Aligns with the Dunford positioning: the differentiator is who you serve, not what you do.

4. **Price positioning: cheaper than answering services at real volume.** Smith.ai is $95/mo at 50 calls but $270-$800 at contractor volume. Ruby starts at $250/50 min. CallLock at $297/mo flat beats both at 150+ calls/month. Lead with the volume comparison, not the entry price.

5. **The after-hours use case is the easiest sell.** Many contractors have adequate coverage during business hours (owner or office manager answers). After hours is where calls go to voicemail universally. "Just use CallLock after 5 PM" is a lower commitment entry point.

## What Could Prove Us Wrong

1. Smith.ai removes the 50-call cap on their AI tier — suddenly $95/mo unlimited AI answering with 7,000+ integrations
2. Ruby or Nexa add AI-native capabilities and drop pricing to compete with Smith.ai
3. Contractors actually prefer human voices over AI for high-value calls ($5,000+ installs) — trust matters more than speed
4. The "books the job" claim requires deeper FSM integration than CallLock currently has — you need to read technician schedules, check service areas, handle job types. If the integration is shallow, you're just another message-taker.
5. Generic answering services are "good enough" for most contractors — they don't need AI, they need any human to pick up

# YC AI Voice Agent Trend Analysis (S23–F25)

Eight YC-backed companies in the AI voice/receptionist space across 5 batches. Here's what the timeline reveals.

## The Companies, Chronologically

| Batch | Company | Vertical | Modality | Scope |
|-------|---------|----------|----------|-------|
| S23 | [[competitors/twine]] | Horizontal SMB | SMS only | Missed call → text response |
| S23 | [[competitors/sameday-ai]] | Home services | Voice | Full AI workforce (receptionist + sales + dispatch) |
| W24 | [[competitors/opencall-ai]] | Horizontal | Voice + text | AI receptionist + booking |
| W24 | [[competitors/arini]] | Dental | Voice | AI receptionist + scheduling |
| W24 | [[competitors/somn]] | Healthcare | Voice | AI receptionist + outbound patient calls |
| S24 | [[competitors/drillbit]] | Home services | Voice + platform | AI receptionist + CRM + quoting + payments |
| F25 | [[competitors/bravi]] | Home services | Voice + chat | AI OS + quoting + lead management |
| W26 | [[competitors/patientdesk-ai]] | Dental | Voice | AI receptionist + insurance + payments + lead gen |

## Trend 1: Horizontal → Vertical → Vertical+

**S23–W24:** Early entrants were either horizontal (Twine, OpenCall) or single-feature vertical (just answering calls). YC funded breadth plays and simple vertical wedges.

**S24–F25:** Every new entrant is vertical-specific AND expanding scope beyond just answering phones. Drillbit adds CRM + quoting + payments. Bravi adds quoting + lead management. Patientdesk adds insurance verification + payment collection + lead gen.

**The pattern:** Start with voice AI as the wedge, then expand into the full workflow. The phone call is the entry point, not the product. The product is owning the entire front-office workflow for a specific vertical.

## Trend 2: "Receptionist" → "Sales Agent" → "AI Workforce"

The positioning evolved over 2 years:
- **S23:** "AI receptionist" / "AI text receptionist" — answering calls
- **W24:** "AI receptionist that books" — answering + action
- **S24:** "AI admin" / "AI employees" — replacing roles
- **F25:** "AI operating system" / "AI workforce" — replacing the entire front office

Each batch raises the ambition. By F25, nobody is pitching "we answer your phone." They're pitching "we run your business."

**Implication for CallLock:** Position as AI workforce for home services from day one. Don't start with "receptionist" framing — you'll be behind the curve.

## Trend 3: Inbound-Only → Inbound + Outbound

- **S23–S24:** Almost entirely inbound (answering calls that come in)
- **W24:** Somn adds outbound (calling patients proactively)
- **F25+:** Bravi adds lead follow-up, Patientdesk adds lead gen campaigns

The market is shifting from reactive to proactive. Companies that only answer inbound calls are leaving revenue on the table.

**Implication for CallLock:** Scout (planned, not yet shipped) targets this gap with signal-based outbound prospecting. Most competitors are inbound-only or just starting basic outbound. If validated with customers, this could be CallLock's biggest differentiation vector.

### Why competitors may deliberately avoid outbound

Before assuming outbound is a "gap," consider why 8 funded companies might skip it on purpose:
- **Regulatory risk:** TCPA, DNC lists, and state-level telemarketing laws create real legal exposure for unsolicited outbound calls. Inbound is legally safe.
- **Conversion economics:** Inbound calls convert at 10-50x higher rates than outbound. Rational companies optimize for the highest-ROI channel first.
- **Brand risk:** Contractors may not want their brand associated with cold calling. "We called you because your AC unit is old" could feel invasive.
- **Different AI model:** Outbound requires persuasion, not service. The conversational AI that books an appointment for a caller is fundamentally different from one that convinces someone to want an appointment.
- **Customer pull vs. push:** Maybe contractors don't want outbound. This is unvalidated — the wiki assumes they do, but zero customer conversations confirm it.

**This is the most important open question for CallLock.** If outbound is a deliberate market decision (not a gap), CallLock's entire thesis needs revision.

## Trend 4: Integration Depth Is the Moat

Every successful vertical player emphasizes integrations:
- Sameday: 5+ field service platforms (ServiceTitan anchor)
- Patientdesk: 15+ dental PMS systems
- Arini: "plugs into existing software"
- Somn: "integrates with fax systems, EHRs" (legacy healthcare infra)
- Drillbit: built its own CRM (different strategy)

The voice AI itself is commoditizing. The moat is how deeply you integrate with the industry-specific tools your customers already use.

**Implication for CallLock:** ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, and Jobber integration is table stakes. The deeper the integration (dispatching, job creation, not just booking), the harder to replace.

## Trend 5: Team Size Is Growing Fast

| Batch | Company | Team at listing |

| S23 | Sameday | Small (not listed) |
| W24 | OpenCall | 9 |

Arini at 17 people (W24) suggests this category can raise and grow fast. Drillbit at 10 (S24) is also well-resourced. The window for bootstrapped entry is closing — funded competitors are scaling teams.

## Trend 6: Founder-Market Fit Wins Deals

Almost every vertical player has founder-market fit:
- Sameday: founder sold phone systems to home services
- Bravi: family runs an installation business
- Somn: founder grew up in mom's medical practice
- Twine: founder sold phone systems to SMBs
- Drillbit: founder renovated homes and Airstreams

YC clearly values this. And customers trust it — "we understand your business because we lived it."

**Implication for CallLock:** If Rashid has direct experience with home service businesses (as a customer, employee, or operator), lead with that story.

## What This Means for CallLock

1. **The category is real and accelerating.** 8 YC companies in 5 batches. This isn't a fad — it's a platform shift.
2. **Outbound may be the gap — or a deliberate market choice.** Almost nobody does signal-based outbound. Scout (planned, not shipped) targets this. But the thesis is unvalidated with customers. See "Why competitors may avoid outbound" above.
3. **Position as "AI workforce" not "AI receptionist."** The market has already moved past the receptionist framing.
4. **Integration depth > voice quality.** The voice AI is table stakes. The moat is workflow integration.
5. **Speed matters.** Teams are growing, categories are filling. The home services vertical has Sameday (W23), Drillbit (S24), and Bravi (F25) already. Each batch adds another competitor.
6. **Pricing data is sparse.** Sameday doesn't publicly disclose pricing (demo-based sales). Patientdesk: 1,500 min/mo base + $0.20/min overage. Most competitors hide pricing behind demos, suggesting enterprise-level price points.

## What Could Prove Us Wrong

- ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, or Jobber ships a "good enough" AI receptionist natively — the integration moat evaporates because the incumbent IS the integration
- Outbound voice AI triggers TCPA enforcement actions, poisoning the category
- Contractors don't want outbound — they want better inbound, and Sameday's 92% booking rate is already "good enough"
- Voice AI quality commoditizes so fast that vertical depth doesn't matter — the cheapest horizontal provider wins
- A well-funded competitor (Sameday, Drillbit) adds outbound before CallLock ships, eliminating the differentiation
